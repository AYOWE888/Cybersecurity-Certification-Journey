# 📘 Summary of Study & Testing

The second module transitioned from identifying **threat actors** to analyzing the specific **Threat Vectors** they utilize. This study focused on the **technical entry points and transmission methods** used to deliver payloads or gain unauthorized access to a network.

## 🧪 Common Threat Vectors

**Focus:** Understanding the **"How"** of a breach.

### 🔑 Key Points

We examined both **digital and physical vectors**, ranging from embedded scripts in image files to the exploitation of trusted **third-party providers**. A heavy emphasis was placed on identifying the risks associated with **legacy systems** and **physical devices** that can bypass logical network defenses.

In cybersecurity analysis, threat vectors can also be evaluated using several key classification dimensions:

### 🧠 Threat Vector Classification Factors

**External vs Internal Vectors**

- **External vectors** originate outside the organization, such as phishing messages, malicious websites, or compromised supply chain providers.
- **Internal vectors** originate within the organization, including insider misuse, unauthorized removable media, or misconfigured systems.

**Technical Sophistication** l;nvwvn; n;dv

- Some vectors rely on **advanced techniques**, including embedded scripts, supply-chain compromises, or multi-stage attacks.
- Others depend on **simple attack methods**, such as default credentials, social engineering, or opportunistic malware delivery.

**Resource Requirements**

- Certain vectors require **extensive infrastructure and planning**, such as supply chain compromises or coordinated campaigns.
- Others require **minimal resources**, relying primarily on human error or misconfiguration to succeed.

Understanding these classifications helps defenders determine **how attacks enter environments**, how likely they are to occur, and how to **prioritize mitigation strategies**.

# 🔎 High Impact Question Analysis

| # | Question Topic | Key Takeaway |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Smishing (SMS Phishing)** | A specific messaging vector where attackers use **text messages** to distribute malicious links, exploiting the high open rates of mobile devices. |
| 2 | **SVG File Risks** | Unlike raster images (JPG/PNG), SVGs are **XML-based code**, allowing attackers to embed malicious JavaScript that executes when rendered in a browser. |
| 3 | **Removable Media (USB)** | USB drops are highly effective against **air-gapped networks**, as they rely on human error to bridge the physical gap between a secure and insecure network. |
| 4 | **Unsupported Systems** | "End of Life" (EOL) systems are high-impact vectors because the manufacturer no longer provides **security patches**, leaving known vulnerabilities permanently exposed. |
| 5 | **802.1X Protocol** | A critical defense against unauthorized hardware, providing **port-based authentication** for both wired and wireless network infrastructure. |
| 6 | **Managed Service Providers** | MSPs represent a major **Supply Chain vector**; a single compromise at the MSP level can grant an attacker access to dozens or hundreds of client networks. |
| 7 | **Default Credentials** | Many IoT and network devices ship with **publicly documented passwords**, making them trivial to compromise if not changed during deployment. |
| 8 | **HID (Human Interface Device)** | Malicious USB devices can emulate **keyboards**, allowing them to automatically "type" commands into a system to bypass traditional security controls. |
