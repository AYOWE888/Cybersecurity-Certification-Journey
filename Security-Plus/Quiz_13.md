# 📘 Summary of Study & Testing

The initial testing phase focused on the **fundamental human elements of cybersecurity**, specifically the different categories of **threat actors** and what drives their behavior. Understanding the **"Who"** and **"Why"** behind cyber attacks is critical for performing effective **risk assessments** and identifying the likely **origins of an attack**.

## 🧪 Test 1: Threat Actors & Motivations

**Focus:** Categorizing external and internal threats.

### 🔑 Key Points

This module explored the distinction between:

- **Structured threats** — such as **Nation-States** and **Organized Crime**, which often operate with clear objectives, significant funding, and long-term strategic goals.
- **Unstructured or opportunistic threats** — such as **Script Kiddies** and **Shadow IT**, which tend to exploit vulnerabilities without sophisticated planning.

We also analyzed the **internal risks posed by employees**, whether those actions are **malicious (intentional)** or **accidental (negligence or lack of awareness)**.

In addition, threat actors can be further analyzed and categorized using several important dimensions commonly referenced in cybersecurity risk frameworks.

### 🧠 Threat Actor Classification Factors

**Internal vs External Actors**

- **Internal actors** originate from within the organization (employees, contractors, or partners).
- **External actors** operate outside the organization (nation-states, cybercriminal groups, hacktivists).

**Level of Sophistication**

- Some actors possess **advanced technical expertise** and use highly customized tools and stealth techniques.
- Others rely on **basic or publicly available exploit tools**.

**Available Resources**

- Certain actors have **extensive resources**, including funding, infrastructure, research teams, and intelligence capabilities.
- Others operate with **minimal resources**, often relying on freely available scripts or exploit kits.

Understanding these classifications allows security teams to better evaluate **risk likelihood**, **potential impact**, and **appropriate defensive strategies**.

# 🔎 High Impact Question Analysis

| # | Question Topic | Key Takeaway |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)** | Typically associated with **Nation-States**, these actors focus on **long-term stealth operations and intelligence gathering** rather than immediate financial gain. |
| 2 | **Shadow IT** | A significant security risk because hardware or software deployed by employees without IT approval remains **unmonitored, unmanaged, and unpatched**. |
| 3 | **Insider Threats** | Particularly dangerous because the individual already has **authorized access to the network** and **knowledge of internal security defenses**. |
| 4 | **Hacktivists** | Their primary motivation is **political or social change**, often utilizing methods such as **website defacement** or **DDoS attacks**. |
| 5 | **Script Kiddies** | These actors typically lack advanced technical expertise and rely on **pre-built tools or scripts created by others** to conduct attacks. |
| 6 | **Organized Crime** | Unlike Nation-State actors, their primary motivation is almost always **financial gain**, frequently through **ransomware operations or large-scale data theft**. |
| 7 | **Technical Motivations** | Some attackers are driven by **reputation, challenge, or curiosity**, seeking recognition within the **hacking or cybersecurity community**. |
| 8 | **Industrial Espionage** | Competitors may conduct cyberattacks to steal **intellectual property, trade secrets, or proprietary technologies** in order to gain a **market advantage**. |
